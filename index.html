About

I am a theoretical physicist and PhD candidate at Purdue University, driven by a deep fascination with the quantum world and its seemingly endless capacity to surprise. What captivates me most is the moment when a collection of simple quantum constituents — electrons, atoms, spins — conspires to produce something qualitatively new: a metal that becomes an insulator, a fluid that spontaneously develops crystalline order, a wire array that chooses superconductivity over disorder. Understanding why these transformations happen, and what controls them, is at the heart of everything I do.

My interests span an unusually wide arc of the quantum landscape. I am fascinated by the deep questions of quantum phase transitions — what makes a phase of matter stable, and what drives it to collapse. I think about why magnets work, about the exotic phases that emerge in correlated electron systems, and about how geometry and disorder, usually treated as nuisances, can be weaponized to engineer materials with enhanced or entirely new properties. I am equally excited by the engineering side: designing metamaterials and quantum devices, developing superconducting nanowire architectures, and thinking about how the machinery of quantum mechanics can be harnessed rather than merely understood.

Beyond the analytic and field-theoretic toolkit I have built over 7+ years — bosonization, renormalization group, mean field theory, random matrix theory — I am passionate about the computational and data-driven side of modern physics. I develop and run Monte Carlo simulations across a range of algorithms, build machine-learning pipelines to extract Hamiltonian parameters and phase boundaries from quantum-material data, and explore how quantum algorithms on devices like Qiskit can begin to simulate processes that classical computers cannot. To me, these are not separate pursuits but different lenses on the same underlying question: what can the quantum world do, and how do we get it to tell us?

My training has taken me across three continents — from Shiv Nadar University in India, to Ludwig Maximilians University in Munich (with Distinction, under Prof. Matthias Punk), to Purdue University where I am a Department of Energy–funded PhD student in the Quantum Materials Theory Group. I have presented my work at international conferences and thrive at the boundary between theory, computation, and experiment.

Research

Coupled Quantum Wires & Emergent Superconductivity In Progress
I study what happens when one-dimensional quantum wires are weakly coupled together and allowed to meander, fluctuate, or become geometrically misaligned. The surprising result is that these geometric effects can suppress competing density-wave tendencies while leaving Josephson coupling alive, making superconducting behavior far more robust than one would expect in a clean array. I use bosonization and renormalization-group methods to turn that idea into a controlled theory, with an eye toward novel superconducting nanowire architectures and photon-detection concepts.
Magnetism, Vortices & Machine Learning In Progress
I am interested in magnetic systems where the important physics is topological rather than simply symmetry-breaking. A classic example is the two-dimensional XY model, where tightly bound vortex–antivortex pairs unbind at a critical temperature and drive the Berezinskii–Kosterlitz–Thouless transition. To connect that physics to realistic data, I combine Monte Carlo simulations with machine-learning models that can learn to identify vortices, defects, and hidden phase boundaries directly from spatial patterns.
Cuprate Physics & Fractional Fermi Liquids
During my master’s work, I studied one of the central puzzles in strongly correlated matter: what really happens when holes are doped into a Mott insulator. In the low-doping pseudogap regime, the system no longer behaves like an ordinary metal, so I worked with a Z2 fractional Fermi-liquid description in which spin and charge reorganize into spinons, holons, and their bound states. Using slave-boson mean-field theory, I derived spin–spin–hole correlations and connected them to signatures seen in quantum gas microscopy experiments, including a striking sign reversal predicted at lower temperatures.
Supersolid Phases in Ultracold Atom Ladders
I also work on extended Bose–Hubbard ladder systems, motivated by tensor-network results showing that a commensurate supersolid can emerge even where a simple one-dimensional chain would not support it. My role is to build the analytical side of that story using bosonization: identifying which competing modes become relevant, why crystalline order and superfluid coherence can coexist, and how re-entrant transitions appear as interactions or rung hopping are tuned.
Quantum Information & Entanglement
I use random-matrix ideas and Floquet dynamics to understand how entanglement builds up in driven quantum systems. In coupled kicked tops, this means asking how the smallest eigenvalues of reduced density matrices behave as the system moves from regular motion into chaos, and how closely those statistics follow Wishart–Laguerre predictions.
High Energy Physics & Scattering Theory
Earlier in my research, I worked on how proton geometry shows up in high-energy scattering processes, especially in exclusive vector-meson production. That project involved comparing different theoretical pictures of the same process and understanding when seemingly different descriptions actually reduce to the same hard-scattering physics.

Electric and Magnetic Field-Dependent Tunneling between Coupled Nanowires
S. Anand, R. Ramachandran, K. Eom et al. — Physical Review B 112, 125415 (2025)  ·  DOI
Abstract

Coupled quasi-one-dimensional (quasi-1D) electron systems host rich emergent physics that cannot be accounted for by understanding isolated 1D electron systems alone. Open questions remain about how transport in these arrays can be manipulated by the application of external electric and magnetic fields. In this theoretical study, we consider a pair of coupled nanowires with non-interacting electrons. We find that a metal-insulator transition is induced by an out-of-plane magnetic field and a transverse potential bias on an array of such coupled wires. We demonstrate the existence of distinct conductance features and highlight the crucial role played by the field dependence of the interwire potential barrier on transport properties. These predictions agree well with transport experiments performed on coupled nanowires sketched on an LaAlO₃/SrTiO₃ interface. Since our model makes minimal assumptions, we expect our predictions to hold for a wide class of coupled 1D systems.

Exclusive Diffractive Vector Meson Production: A Comparison between the Dipole Model and the Leading Twist Shadowing Approach
S. Anand, T. Toll — Physical Review C 100, 024901 (2019)  ·  DOI
Abstract

With the imminent construction of an electron-ion collider in the USA, both saturation physics and nuclear shadowing physics will be important for the same processes for the first time. In particular for exclusive production of vector mesons, such as the J/ψ. In this paper we investigate the underlying assumptions commonly made when phenomenologically investigating this process from a shadowing and saturation perspective. We use the bSat model which is commonly used to describe saturation physics, and a calculation by M.G. Ryskin which is commonly used when describing nuclear shadowing at leading twist. It is expected that the bSat model is equivalent to Ryskin’s result in the hard scattering and non-relativistic limits. By explicitly taking these limits we show that the bSat model does indeed become equivalent to Ryskin’s result. However, two more approximations are needed for this result. Firstly, the factorisation and renormalisation scales in the bSat model has to be independent of the dipole radius, and secondly, we need to omit a term in the overlap between the vector meson and virtual photon wave functions. We show that for the typical dipole radius of the J/ψ, the different approximations off-set each other and the bSat model agrees very well with Ryskin’s calculation in hard-scattering and non-relativistic limits.

Efficient Implementation of the Wang-Landau Algorithm for Systems with Length-Scalable Potential Energy Functions
S. Kumar, G. Kumar, R. S. Chandramouli, S. Anand — Physical Review E 98, 063301 (2018)  ·  DOI
Abstract

We consider a class of systems where N identical particles with positions q₁,…,qₙ and momenta p₁,…,pₙ are enclosed in a box of size L, and exhibit the scaling U(Lr₁,…,Lrₙ)=α(L) U(r₁,…,rₙ) for the associated potential energy function U(q₁,…,qₙ). For these systems, we propose an efficient implementation of the Wang-Landau algorithm for evaluating thermodynamic observables involving energy and volume fluctuations in the microcanonical description, and temperature and volume fluctuations in the canonical description. This requires performing the Wang-Landau simulation in a scaled box of unit size and evaluating the density of states corresponding to the potential energy part only. To demonstrate the efficacy of our approach, as example systems, we consider Padmanabhan’s binary star model and an ideal gas trapped in a harmonic potential within the box.

Smallest Eigenvalue Density for Regular or Fixed-Trace Complex Wishart-Laguerre Ensemble and Entanglement in Coupled Kicked Tops
S. Kumar, B. Sambasivam, S. Anand — Journal of Physics A 50, 345201 (2017)  ·  DOI
Abstract

The statistical behaviour of the smallest eigenvalue has important implications for systems which can be modeled using a Wishart-Laguerre ensemble, the regular one or the fixed trace one. For example, the density of the smallest eigenvalue of the Wishart-Laguerre ensemble plays a crucial role in characterizing multiple channel telecommunication systems. Similarly, in the quantum entanglement problem, the smallest eigenvalue of the fixed trace ensemble carries information regarding the nature of entanglement. For real Wishart-Laguerre matrices, there exists an elegant recurrence scheme suggested by Edelman to directly obtain the exact expression for the smallest eigenvalue density. In the case of complex Wishart-Laguerre matrices, for finding exact and explicit expressions for the smallest eigenvalue density, existing results based on determinants become impractical when the determinants involve large-size matrices. In this work, we derive a recurrence scheme for the complex case which is analogous to that of Edelman’s for the real case. This is used to obtain exact results for the smallest eigenvalue density for both the regular, and the fixed trace complex Wishart-Laguerre ensembles. We validate our analytical results using Monte Carlo simulations. We also study scaled Wishart-Laguerre ensemble and investigate its efficacy in approximating the fixed-trace ensemble. Eventually, we apply our result for the fixed-trace ensemble to investigate the behaviour of the smallest eigenvalue in the paradigmatic system of coupled kicked tops.

Geometric Disorder, Sliding Phases, and Enhanced Superconductivity in Coupled Quantum Wire Arrays
S. Anand et al. — manuscript in preparation
Abstract

Coupled one-dimensional electron fluids provide a controlled route to unconventional two-dimensional phases. While the renormalization-group stability of sliding phases in clean coupled-wire arrays is well understood for spin-gapped (Luther–Emery) systems, less is known about the fate of these RG flows in the presence of geometric perturbations—meandering, random forward-scattering, and related sources of longitudinal dephasing intrinsic to fluctuating stripe textures. We develop a renormalization group mechanism to study fluctuating disordered stripes as well as stripes that possess a unique but mutually incommensurate wavelength. In the former, a dimensional crossover is predicted that modifies the irrelevance conditions for charge-density-wave locking as well as single and multiple transverse hopping processes, but leaving the Josephson couplings intact. In the latter, a commensurate–incommensurate transition is predicted which kills all such coupling terms at long wavelengths except Josephson coupling. Such constructions make the sliding Luttinger liquid phase and the superconducting regime a far more robust phase of matter than expected for the clean counterparts. We demonstrate a robust sliding phase in a minimal two-wire architecture which is expressly prohibited in clean arrays. Furthermore, we demonstrate that any geometric disorder or detuning is always relevant and the system inevitably flows to a strongly dephased state for sufficiently long wavelengths.

Commensurate Supersolids and Re-entrant Transitions in an Extended Bose–Hubbard Ladder
A. N. Madhusudan, G. C. Santra, I. Kaur, W. Li, R. Nath (+ S. Anand, forthcoming)
Abstract

We investigate the ground state phases of an extended Bose-Hubbard ladder of unit filling via the density-matrix-renormalization-group method and, in particular, the effect of rung-hoppings. In contrast to a single-chain, a commensurate supersolid emerges, and based on the Luttinger parameter, we classify them into two types. The latter leads to a reentrant gapless behavior as the onsite interaction is increased while keeping all other parameters intact. A reentrant gapped transition is also found as a function of nearest-neighbor interactions. Further, we show that the string order characterizing the Haldane phase vanishes for a finite inter-chain hopping amplitude, however small it is. Finally, we propose two experimental platforms to observe our findings, using either dipolar atoms or polar molecules and Rydberg admixed atoms.
